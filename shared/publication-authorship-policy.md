# Publication & Authorship Policy

Big Bioinformatics Lab (BBL) Bangladesh is building a reputation for high-quality,
well-presented science. Authorship is both **credit** and **accountability**: every author
must be able to stand behind the work. This policy sets clear, enforceable rules for who
becomes an author, in what order, and how disputes are handled. It applies to every
manuscript, preprint, conference abstract, poster, and dataset released under the lab's name.

This is a **living document**. Propose changes via pull request (see the
[Lab Guidelines](/shared/center-guidelines.md)).

---

# 1. Principles

- **Merit, not status.** Authorship reflects intellectual contribution, not seniority,
  funding, or position in the lab.
- **Earned, not gifted.** Gift, guest, and ghost authorship are prohibited.
- **Decided early, revisited often.** Author roles are discussed at project start and
  reviewed as contributions change.
- **Transparent.** Each author's specific contribution is recorded (see
  [CRediT](#5-contributor-roles-credit)).
- **Accountable.** Each author is responsible for the integrity of the whole work and can
  identify which co-authors are responsible for which parts.

---

# 2. Who Qualifies as an Author

BBL follows the
[ICMJE Uniform Requirements](https://www.icmje.org/recommendations/browse/roles-and-responsibilities/defining-the-role-of-authors-and-contributors.html).

## 2.1 ICMJE Criteria

Authorship requires **all four** of the following:

1. Substantial contribution to **conception or design**, or **acquisition, analysis, or
   interpretation** of data; **and**
2. **Drafting** the work or **revising** it critically for important intellectual content; **and**
3. **Final approval** of the version to be published; **and**
4. **Agreement to be accountable** for all aspects of the work, ensuring questions of
   accuracy or integrity are investigated and resolved.

Meeting criterion 1 alone is not enough. Everyone who meets all four criteria must be
listed; everyone listed must meet all four.

## 2.2 Contributions That Do *Not* Qualify

These earn an [acknowledgment](#6-acknowledgments), not authorship:

- Acquiring funding alone.
- General supervision of the research group alone.
- Providing data, samples, reagents, or compute access alone.
- Technical help, language editing, or proofreading alone.

A contributor who does not meet all four criteria but did meaningful work should be named
in the Acknowledgments with their specific role.

---

# 3. Author Order

Author order is proposed by the project lead and the PI/mentor and agreed by all authors.
Default conventions for BBL:

## 3.1 First Author
The person who did the majority of the work — typically the lead student or analyst who
designed and ran the analysis and drafted the manuscript.

## 3.2 Last / Senior Author
The PI/mentor who provided overall direction, resources, and supervision.

## 3.3 Middle Authors
Ordered by magnitude of contribution, agreed among the authors. Roughly equal contributors
may be listed alphabetically — state this in the author notes if so.

## 3.4 Shared Positions
Co-first or co-senior authorship is allowed when contributions are genuinely comparable.
Mark with an equal-contribution footnote. Decide the order of equal contributors explicitly
and record it in writing.

---

# 4. Corresponding Author

- One corresponding author is named per manuscript (a second is allowed if a journal permits).
- Responsibilities: manuscript submission, communication with the journal, responding to
  reviewers, handling post-publication queries, and ensuring all co-authors approve the
  final version and any revisions.
- The corresponding author maintains the up-to-date contact details and ORCID iDs of all
  authors. **Every author must have an [ORCID](https://orcid.org/) iD.**

---

# 5. Contributor Roles (CRediT)

Every manuscript records each author's contribution using the
[CRediT taxonomy](https://credit.niso.org/) (14 roles):

Conceptualization · Data curation · Formal analysis · Funding acquisition · Investigation ·
Methodology · Project administration · Resources · Software · Supervision · Validation ·
Visualization · Writing – original draft · Writing – review & editing.

Maintain a CRediT table in the project repository from day one and update it as the project
evolves. This table is the basis for author-order discussions and dispute resolution.

| Author | Roles |
|--------|-------|
| *Name* | *e.g., Conceptualization, Software, Writing – original draft* |

---

# 6. Acknowledgments

Name contributors who did not meet authorship criteria, with their role (e.g., "provided
sequencing data," "advised on statistics"). **Obtain written permission** before naming
anyone, since acknowledgment can imply endorsement. Funding sources and grant numbers go here.

---

# 7. When Authorship Is Decided

- **Project start:** Draft the expected author list, tentative order, and CRediT table.
  Record it in the project repo.
- **During the project:** Revisit whenever contributions shift materially (someone leaves,
  someone takes on a major new piece of work).
- **Before submission:** Confirm the final list and order, complete the CRediT table, and
  collect every author's [sign-off](#15-authorship-agreement-sign-off).
- **Abstracts/posters:** Per the Lab Guidelines, share with all co-authors (incl. mentors)
  **at least one week** before the deadline; posters in `#general` **at least a week** before
  printing.

Early discussion prevents most disputes. Document decisions — informal agreements are not enough.

**Minimum-commitment contingency.** CHIRAL positions are voluntary and carry a minimum active
term (see [Center Guidelines → Appointment, Commitment & Affiliation](/shared/center-guidelines.md#appointment-commitment--affiliation)).
A member who departs or goes inactive before completing that term, without the director's
agreement, **forfeits authorship on any work that does not yet independently meet all four
[ICMJE criteria](#21-icmje-criteria)** at the time of departure; such contributions move to the
Acknowledgments. Completing the committed term does not by itself confer authorship — the ICMJE
criteria must still be met.

---

# 8. Preprints, Data, and Code

- **Preprints encouraged.** Post to an appropriate server (e.g., bioRxiv, arXiv, medRxiv)
  with all co-authors' agreement. All authors approve the preprint version, same as for a
  manuscript.
- **Code reviewed before release.** All analysis code is reviewed before a preprint or
  manuscript is submitted (see [Lab Guidelines → Reproducibility](/shared/center-guidelines.md)).
- **Data and code availability.** Deposit generated data in the relevant repository
  (e.g., GEO, SRA) and release code under the lab's default license
  (**BSD-2-Clause Plus Patent**, unless a sponsor requires otherwise). Include a Data
  Availability and Code Availability statement in every manuscript.
- **Licensing/IP concerns** are raised with the director before submission.

# 9. Revision & Resubmission Responsibilities

A manuscript is not finished when it is submitted. Most papers come back from the journal
with reviewer comments, and the response to those comments often decides whether the paper
is accepted. **Revision is part of authorship, not an optional extra.** Too often the
revision burden falls on one or two people while other authors disengage — this is not
acceptable at BBL and is treated as a participation lapse under
[Section 11](#11-accountability--penalty-ladder).

## 9.1 Every Author Must Contribute to Revisions

- When reviewer comments arrive, the **corresponding author** circulates them to all authors
  within **3 working days** and assigns each author the comments that fall in their area
  (using the [CRediT table](#5-contributor-roles-credit)).
- Every author is **required** to respond to the comments assigned to them — new analyses,
  rewriting, new figures, or a written reply — by the internal deadline.
- "I am busy" is not an exemption. If an author genuinely cannot contribute to a round of
  revision, they must say so **in writing immediately**, and their authorship is reviewed
  under [Section 9.3](#93-how-revision-work-affects-authorship).
- Silence is treated as failure to contribute, not as approval.

## 9.2 Internal Revision Deadlines

- The corresponding author sets an **internal deadline** that is at least **5 working days
  before the journal deadline**, to leave time to assemble, proofread, and approve the full
  response.
- Authors deliver their assigned parts by the internal deadline. Drafts submitted late force
  the rest of the team to absorb the work and are logged as a lapse.
- The full response and revised manuscript circulate to **all authors for final approval**
  before resubmission — the same approval rule as first submission
  ([ICMJE criterion 3](#21-icmje-criteria)).

## 9.3 How Revision Work Affects Authorship

Contribution to revision is a direct test of [ICMJE criteria 2 and 4](#21-icmje-criteria)
(revising the work, and accountability). Therefore:

- An author who **does not contribute** to a substantial revision, after assignment and a
  written reminder, no longer meets criterion 2 for the revised work. Their authorship is
  reviewed and may be **moved down in order** or **moved to the Acknowledgments**.
- An author who **picks up another's abandoned revision work** may move **up** in author order
  to reflect the real contribution. Author order is based on actual contribution, including
  revision work — not the order set at first submission.
- All such changes follow [Section 11](#11-accountability--penalty-ladder) and require the
  written-consent process in [Section 12](#12-changing-the-author-list).

# 10. Participation, Attendance & Communication

Publication is a team effort that depends on people showing up and answering. Two recurring
problems are addressed here directly: **missed lab meetings** and **slow communication**.
These are not separate from publication — they are how revisions stall and deadlines get
missed. Lapses are counted and escalated under [Section 11](#11-accountability--penalty-ladder).

## 10.1 Lab Meeting Attendance

- The weekly lab meeting is **every Thursday, 9:30–11:00**. Attendance is **mandatory** for
  all lab members.
- A member who cannot attend must notify the meeting lead and the `#meeting` Zulip channel
  **at least 24 hours in advance**, with a reason.
- **Excused absence:** advance notice with a valid reason (illness, exam, prior approved
  commitment). No penalty.
- **Unexcused absence:** no advance notice, or no valid reason. Counts as **one lapse**.
- A member who is absent must read the meeting notes and catch up on action items before the
  next meeting — being absent does not remove their revision or project deadlines.

## 10.2 Timely Communication

- **Routine project communication** (Zulip, email, PR reviews): respond within **48 hours**.
- **Active revision or an approaching submission deadline:** respond within **24 hours**.
- **Abstracts/posters:** the existing Lab Guidelines rule still applies — share with all
  co-authors **at least one week** before the deadline.
- An author who goes silent (no response within the window) on a message that needs their
  input is logged as **one lapse**, and the work proceeds without them — including, where
  needed, reassigning their revision tasks per [Section 9.3](#93-how-revision-work-affects-authorship).

## 10.3 Logging Lapses

- The corresponding author (for a given paper) or the meeting lead (for attendance) records
  each lapse with the date and a one-line reason, in the project repo or the lab's tracking
  sheet.
- The member is told **at the time** that a lapse has been logged — no surprise penalties.
- Lapses are counted **per rolling 6-month period**, then reset.

# 11. Accountability & Penalty Ladder

This ladder applies to the participation lapses defined in
[Sections 9 and 10](#9-revision--resubmission-responsibilities): missed revision contributions,
unexcused missed meetings, and missed communication windows. It is graded — penalties escalate
with repetition — and is consistent with the escalation in the
[Social Media Policy](/shared/social-media-policy.md).

## 11.1 Penalty Ladder

| Lapses (rolling 6 months) | Consequence |
|---|---|
| **1st** | Verbal reminder from the corresponding author or meeting lead, logged. Member catches up on the missed item. |
| **2nd** | Written warning (logged in the project record), and a written catch-up plan with a deadline. The PI/mentor is informed. |
| **3rd** | **Authorship review.** The member's contribution is re-checked against [ICMJE criteria](#21-icmje-criteria). Possible outcomes: move down in author order, or move to the Acknowledgments if criteria 2/4 are no longer met. Decided by the PI/mentor with the corresponding author. |
| **Persistent / severe** | Removal from the authorship and the project, and referral to the director. For a member, this may feed into the broader association review in the Lab Guidelines. |

- A lapse during an **active journal revision** is weighted more heavily, because it directly
  risks the submission — the corresponding author may escalate one step faster.
- Penalties affect **only the lapsing member's** standing. They never reduce the credit of
  members who did the work.

## 11.2 Resolution Procedure

1. **Log and notify.** The lapse is recorded and the member is told immediately
   ([Section 10.3](#103-logging-lapses)).
2. **Direct conversation.** The corresponding author (or meeting lead) and the member discuss
   it, using the log and the CRediT table as the factual basis. Most cases end here.
3. **Mentor.** If unresolved, or at the 3rd lapse, the PI/mentor reviews and decides the
   authorship outcome.
4. **Director.** If still unresolved, or for removal from authorship, escalate to the director,
   whose decision is final within the lab.
5. **Document everything.** Each step is recorded with dates in the project record. Decisions
   are based on contribution, not seniority or personal relationships.

## 11.3 Appeals

A member may appeal a penalty in writing to the PI/mentor within **7 days**, or to the
director if the PI/mentor is involved in the dispute. The appeal must reference the logged
record. The director's decision is final.

---

# 12. Changing the Author List

Adding, removing, or reordering authors after the list is agreed requires **written
consent from all current authors**, including the person added or removed. The
corresponding author keeps the record. After submission, the journal's authorship-change
procedure must also be followed. Changes that result from a participation lapse follow
[Section 11](#11-accountability--penalty-ladder) but still use this written-consent process.

---

# 13. Dispute Resolution

1. **Talk first.** The authors involved attempt to resolve it among themselves, using the
   CRediT table as the factual basis.
2. **Mediator.** If unresolved, the PI/mentor (or, if the PI is a party to the dispute, an
   uninvolved senior lab member) mediates.
3. **Director.** If still unresolved, escalate to the director, whose decision is final
   within the lab.
4. **Honesty over harmony.** Disputes are resolved on the merits of contribution, not
   seniority or personal relationships.

Raise concerns early — do not wait until submission.

---

# 14. Misconduct

Prohibited and treated as misconduct under the lab's ethics expectations:

- **Gift/guest authorship** — listing someone who did not meet the criteria.
- **Ghost authorship** — omitting someone who did meet the criteria.
- **Plagiarism**, data fabrication, or falsification.
- **Duplicate or salami submission** without disclosure.

Consequences follow the [Lab Guidelines](/shared/center-guidelines.md) and Code of Conduct,
up to removal from the project and termination of association. Illegal activity is reported
to the appropriate authorities.

---

# 15. Authorship Agreement (Sign-Off)

Before any submission, every author signs the statement below (a Google Form or a signed
note in the project repo is acceptable). Keep the record with the project.

> **I confirm that:**
> 1. I meet all four ICMJE authorship criteria for this work.
> 2. The author list, author order, and CRediT contributions as recorded are accurate.
> 3. I approve the version to be submitted.
> 4. I take responsibility for the integrity of the work and will help investigate and
>    resolve any questions of accuracy or integrity.
> 5. The work is original, not plagiarized, and not under consideration elsewhere
>    (unless disclosed).
